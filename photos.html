Using deep learning technologies to bring old photos back to life

Family photos play an important part of any family’s history, and as such, we have an entire section in the product dedicated to preserving and sharing photos.

As covid kicked in, we started seeing growing usage in our photos’ section. People were being nostalgic. We wanted to build on that, and take it to the next level. What photo features will give users the most value? What will create ongoing excitement, and give people some comfort during these times?

We dived into our research...

The Team

As Director of Design, I gathered my talented team of 6 Product Designers and Researches to work on these features simultaneously, each responsible for their own domain: the web and mobile web products, the iOS and Android native applications, and the acquisition flows promoting these upcoming features. Each Product Designer worked closely with a Product Manager and an independent team of multiple FE and BE developers, QA’s and Data Analysts.

Worked on by six parallel and connecting domains, with product teams spread across the globe, managing a synced process and creating a coherent experience can be challenging. From past projects’ learnings, it was clear to me that from the get go, decisions need to be made looking at all flows and platforms, striving for consistency, and speeding up development by reusing behaviours, flows and components.

I set clear communication channels and 3 weekly sync meetings involving all relevant stakeholders, as we began our research...

UX Research

I sent the team on their first research mission, to analyze the photos people were interacting with, and talk to the users themselves, getting a better understanding of their needs. We found out that people were mostly interacting with their old nostalgic photos. Photos being mostyle in black and white, or sepia, and of poor quality, the demand for editing options and photo filters stood out.

But what can differentiate our solution from all other photo editing tools? How can we answer our users’ needs in a way that gives them extra value? We started looking into open source libraries that offer automated coloring algorithms, and tested hundreads of historical photos. The results looked promising.

Striving for user-centered processes, I asked the team to check how real users respond to their own old family photos, now in color. To our satisfaction, the reactions were incredible. People were delighted, asking to see more and more of their photos being colorized. We were heading in the right direction.

As users were zooming in on the newly colored photos, trying to see every little detail, the same feedback repeated itself. Users were frustrated with the quality of their old photos, hoping their blurry and faded photos could be improved. We repeated the same process, of technical research, testing different algorithms, and interviewing users with their own sharpened photos to gather feedback. The algorithm’s results were impressive, and users were thrilled.

Users were excited and emotional seeing loved ones in a whole new perspective.
wow!
UX Solution & Release

Being straight forward and automatic features, I strived for an experience as simple as possible. Two action icons were added to the bottom of the photo screen, allowing to easily trigger the effects.

As a mantra I keep repeating to the team, when conducting user tests, always pay attention to what users are actually doing, how they are acting, even more than what they are saying. As we saw people extensively zooming in on their newly enhanced photos, going back and forth between the original and improved versions, comparing the two, we wanted to build on that and add some playfulness to the comparison. We added a simple slider that people can play with, and focus on specific areas of the photo.

After another round of tests, and small tweaks, both features were released, and we followed usage metrics. In the first few days, as existing users started noticing the new features, engagement started increasing. Next Step

We wanted to build on that momentum, and push it further. If people were so excited about their old family photos coming back to life through colorization and sharpening, how would they feel if we really breathed life into them?

We started playing with deep fake technologies, and immediately knew this would be trickier, from a user adoption perspective. The results were sometimes a little off, and we had to gather substantial user feedback to understand if people get creeped out when seeing an old ancestor suddenly move in front of them. As opposed to previous user interview sessions, we received mixed reviews, but to our surprise, the majority of feedback was positive, and that gave us the green light to go ahead.

And so, we released our third photo effect feature, named Deep Nostalgia
We released the feature, and as usage was slowly increasing, I guided the team to follow social media posts as it became a great source for following users’ reactions, frustrations and requests. An example for such a request was to add new animation options, and so we did.
Usage was spiking
Little did we know, what a viral sensation Deep Nostalgia will turn out to be. The MyHeritage App ranked 1st in the U.S App store, and 7th on Google Play, and was mentioned all over. From a segment on Jimmy Kimmel’s Late Night Show, to featured articles on every major news and tech platform, to millions of people posting their ancestors’ videos and reactions on social media, Deep Nostalgia was everywhere.
