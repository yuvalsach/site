Creating the company’s first Design System

It was 2017, and MyHeritage, a leading international genealogy startup, was about to enter a new adventure. Founded in 2003, spanning across dozens of different products and growing into a 500 employees’ organization, the company was making its first steps into the healthtech domain. With home DNA test kits spreading across the industry, expanding the kit’s scientific abilities and combining it with family history details, could create interesting health insights, and opportunities.

Scientific staff was hired, research has began, and all FDA related bureaucracies were in full motion. A hard deadline was set, as we wanted to be first-to-market among our industry. As we had to move fast, we decided to reuse whatever possible from our existing products.

So began the long and winding road towards the company’s first ever Design System.

The Team
In my position at the time as Director of Product Design, I was the initiator and manager of the two year process. What started in leading 2 Senior Product Designers throughout the design process of the Health product, turned into leading a company rebrand process and a full blown Design System creation, involving the entire Design team of 8 Product Designers and 2 Marketing Designers, Management, 2 outsource design agencies, and the full involvement of Product, Marketing and R&D departments.
The Challenge
As often happens in large organizations, with extensive code and product legacy, I had to overcome the challenges new Design Systems usually face: from driving management buy-in by raising awareness to the current problems and the great possible long term value, to getting sufficient resources for building a dedicated team, implementing new work processes and tools, leading organization-wide training and education workshops, incorporating the Design System into the company’s roadmap with measurable KPI’s and goals, and ongoing improvements of all of the above.
But a few steps back, to where it all started, designing the new Health product...
Brand Brothers
As a product that is an extension of the current ancestry DNA kit services, it was clear that the brands should be related, creating a coherent product offering. As such, the entire visual language, the logo, the physical kit and all marketing materials were developed further, given a more scientific and trust-worthy feel, yet keeping alignment and communication between the two products.

Reuse, Reuse, Reuse

Product Design process aside, from a UI perspective, I pushed the team into reusing existing designs and behaviours, allowing R&D to build everything as fast as possible. In addition to the brand ties, the DNA product was the most up-to-date product, from both design and code perspectives, so we decided to build on that.

All UX behaviours, page structures, basic components, iconography, typography and style standards, were mapped and organized, to be easily reused, and expanded when needed, as we we were building the very first building blocks of our repository. The brand’s different primary color was the only differentiation between the two products.

However, In parallel, many marketing and visual materials were being made for the upcoming release, some in external agencies, and the lack of a clear brand strategy and brand guidelines was hurting us.
The inconsistency between the three products was rough...

The MyHeritage Story

It wasn’t easy stopping the many moving parts that were in motion, but I realized that we had to stop and create a coherent brand strategy, a story that connects the three main product domains. We collaborated with our Marketing and Business Development departments, and after a few workshops and different directions that were tested, we finalized the brand story: Empowering users to connect their past (Genealogy), present (DNA) and future (Health).

With this decision in mind, I gathered the design team for a branding offsite, that stretched across a few days of endless brainstorming, sketching, collaborating, debating, and agreeing. We finally locked into our preferred direction, and clear values were defined for each domain: the warmth and familyhood of the genealogy products, the diversity and excitement of the DNA product, and the scientific, humane empowerment of the health product. These values will be represented by playful sceneries, combining fitting cut-out imagery, lively illustrations, and our dominant brush stroke element. The current primary brand colors will be kept as an important differentiator between the domains, and a new cross-domain wide color palette will be added.

Being analogical in a digital world
Genealogy is a very nostalgic topic, consisting of old documents, photos, archives, all giving us a glimpse into how our ancestors used to live. In contrast to the more modern DNA and Health products, we wanted to have a subtle distinction to this domain, emphasizing that analogical and nostalgic feel. We added old-time objects as the main element in the icons and illustrations of this domain, adding the desired distinction and twist we were looking for.
I just had to animate some of these as well...
Next Steps
Now that the brand was taking shape, and clearer guidelines for the upcoming Health product were set, R&D were moving fast. The initial guides and components alignment and reuse we’ve defined were proving themselves to be beneficial, and management was surprised by the speed of development. The buzzwords ‘Design Systems’ and ‘Components’ were trending in the company hallways (mainly because my team and I were whispering them all over), and I knew this was a great opportunity to push forward with a Design System initiative, after a few unsuccessful past attempts.

I dived deeper into our product design audit. All structures, layouts, components, behaviors and atoms that were in common use, or had the potential of being in common use, were mapped, aligned and specced, based on current and future possible usages, our early set of guides, and common industry standards.

At this point, I asked the team to test these decisions on the older, yet centric genealogy products, among them the family tree editor, the historical records’ search engine and the automatic matches. The results looked promising.

I pushed R&D to be involved in this process from the get go, rightfully making them feel a leading part of the process, acknowledging their importance, contribution and responsibility. Their excitement and willingness to push this initiative forward, will prove itself to be a game changer. In addition, I wanted our code and design repositories to be identical, fully aligned and code based, and as such, the mutual collaboration around components’ structure, props and responsiveness was crucial. What started in Sketch and later on migrated to Figma were smart, content aware, responsive and interactive components that were handed off to R&D, that built them in React, documenting everything on Storybook.
We kept on diving deeper into the process, as more complex and company specific components and specs were being added to the Design System.
Resolution

In parallel, many new features were being worked on in various product domains. Management saw how fast and consistent everything was being designed, built and shipped, and R&D efficiency graphs made it crystal clear: the Design System, although very young, was already having a great positive impact on the organization.

However, as time passed on by, that impact started to wear out. Limited resources, release deadlines, and tight product roadmaps were the realities controlling the situation. Our Storybook repository quickly became a mess, full of half-built components that weren’t serving anyone, as components were being partially built and duplicated for slightly different variations, and local component overrides were hurting our progress and ability to maintain the entire system. The gaps between Figma and Storybook were growing steadily, as R&D didn’t have the resources to catch up with Design deliverables.

We were in a bad position, chasing our own tail trying to organize everything that was being built, but it was clear to me that we had to drastically change our work processes, and resource allocation. I suggested we run a comparison to understand how much of our Design System had actually been implemented in Storybook, and a staggering 50% result, proved that things had to change.

What started as a short Technical Sprint involving all FE developers to start closing these gaps, quickly turned into a steady structural change, aligning with what I have been advocating for for years, dedicated FE Design System resources. Although not a unified independent team, we made the decision that each product domain would allocate a few team members that spend the majority of their time on our Design System. We defined clear rules for what a component has to contain, set override and duplication bans, and clear communication channels and documentation methods.

We continue to tweak and improve the processes and communication on an ongoing basis, and two years since it all began, the company now has a well structured decentralized Design System team and process, embedded into the company's goals and roadmaps, maintaining and developing the Design System as the company grows. The company has seen indescribable improvements in design and development time to market and ux consistency across all products. With recent viral AI Avatars and Deep Learning features we have shipped, our Design System played a pivotal role in our ability to ship these products fast and be first-to-market.

What a journey it has been.
