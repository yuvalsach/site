Revamping the Family Tree Editor

The core of any family history research is the family tree. It is more than just a visual representation of family branches, dating back to great great ancestores, it is the rooted structure that holds the family stories together. The big events, and the small nostalgic anecdotes.

As such, people spend most of their time in the Family Tree Editor, adding new information, revisiting past content, diving deeper into their research. It was time to revamp the Family Tree Editor, and bring it up to it’s full potential...

UX Research

With over 3 million monthly users visiting their family tree, two major points were clear to me. First, there was a lot of data for us to base decisions on, and second, every small product change we’ll make will have to be measured carefully, making sure we don’t upset users, and don’t hurt key metrics. ‏‏‎Both of these points meant ‏‏‎ ‎we‏‏‎ ‎ could ‏‏‎ ‎lead a truly data-driven process.

I asked our Data Analysts and FE developers to add all necessary activities and data points via Google Analytics, Hotjar, and Tableu. We looked into the most common user flows, which parts of the flow encouraged further engagement, and which was leading to higher bounce rates. We sorted and prioritized the dozens of actionable features available on the screen, based on usage and monitization metrics.

We had rich user feedback pouring in from all platforms, as we conducted user tests and interviews, gathered support department tickets and social media mentions. Combining both the qualitative and the quantitative research insights, made it clear we were looking at three very distinct personas:

The Novice

First steps of researching their family history and building their family tree
  • In need of easy tools to add relatives, facts and photos
  • In need of learning the entire system and it’s benefits
The Family Explorer
In a constant drive to find new and meaningful information
  • In need of breaking the barrier of the information they already know
  • In need of learning new and more advanced research tools
The Genealogist
Managing large detailed family trees dating centuries back
  • In need of researching specific tree clusters, locations and dates
  • In need of spotting the gaps and mistakes within their trees

We love adding improvements and new features for our ‘Genealogist’ loyal user base, but I was making the case to first tackle the problems Novice and Family Explorer users were facing, being a distinct majority of users, and knowing the amazing value we could offer them. I wanted to help them in their first few steps and ongoing research, by delivering immediate matches and insights.

With our matching technology, as users add additional relatives, facts and photos, the system automatically searches for new information, hidden within other family trees and historical records. Data showed that users become much more engaged once they start receiving matches. I wanted to help them get matches, fast.

I guided the team to focus on these 2 personas, and as research deepened, dozens of pain points and usability issues were found, analyzed and prioritized. 3 main issues kept appearing:

The Old Family Tree Editor
  • ‏‎Users were confused by the amount of actions ‎presented to them on the screen
  • Users were having trouble navigating within their tree, ‎finding specific relatives and branches
  • ‎Large areas of the canvas were obstructed by ‎‎distracting and low usage elements

but most importantly...

Users were not sure what to do next, what actionable steps they can, and should make...

Solution and Beta Test
On to the drawing board, we started brainstorming on a few ideas. I’m a strong believer in clear, minimalistic experiences, and pushed the team to strive for clarity, and contextual guidance, leaning on known theories as the Pareto Principle and the Progressive Disclosure pattern. We cleaned the canvas, and the canvas cards. Low usage actions were removed, or moved to secondary flows, and contextual actions were flushed out, based on parameters such as user type or tree size. After iterating on a few additional ideas, we decided to create a Person Panel, with relevant information and actions:

• Basic personal info, keeps focus and context

• Search person in historical records database

• Main actions, helping users add and edit info

• Flush out any automatic matches, or tree errors

• Prompt DNA services (or show results if tested)

• Tagged photos, prompting to photo features

• Personal timeline, users can add to and edit

Prototype, test, tweak, repeat. The team did an amazing job iterating and improving the initial directions, and after a positive Beta run, we were getting ready to ship our first stage of the A/B test, exposing the new design to a small subset of new users.

We set all measuerable goals we wanted to follow, UX goals and monetizable activities. Among the activities that require a subscription are saving historical records, extracting data from other trees, contacing other site owners, and many more. We knew that for non-subscribed users, getting immediate value will create additional engagement, that will lead to additional value, that will spark motivation and curiosity to take their research to the next level.

The new variant was performing nicely, and small tweaks were made along the way, as we increased exposure. After a few days, exposure was up to 100%, and all new users were getting the new design.

We were now ready to test the new tree with our loyal existing users, that have been using the same tool for almost 10 years! And some, being elderly, do not adjust to change easily. We eased them into it with an introductory experience, walking them through the new design. We let them know they could switch to the old design from the settings panel. We gradually exposed the new design, and in addition to the metrics coming in, we paid close attention to all social media mentions. We were there for them and answered any feedback shared. Fact was, our existing users loved the new version, and the metrics reflected exactly that. In addition to the increased engagement and conversion, only 0.5% of existing users wanted to keep using the old design.

However, this was only the first step. Some of the pain points we initialy discovered our advanced users were facing, and that they were still facing after we released the new design, could not be easily handled without complicating the entire view.

These users were asking for ways to interact with different subsets of their tree, and as we deepened our research, and prototyped a few versions, we concluded that the classic family tree view had it’s limits, and we should keep it simple, for the majority of it’s users. To answer our advanced users’ needs, we started researching additional tree views, and after many cycles of ideation, prototyping, testing and tweaking, we ended up creating three additional tree views.

List View

Users wanted to be able to view relatives in different contexts, e.g. see all relatives from a specific country, or that were born in a specific century.

A list representation, with filters, sorting and searching functionalities was the power tool they were looking for.

Pedigree View

Users wanted to focus on specific ancestral branches within their tree, to easily research back in time.

Presenting only direct ancestral connections, created a clean and easy-to-trace canvas.

Fan View

As users were now focusing on specific ancestral branches, tracing back many generations into the past, they wanted a quick and clear way to view missing individuals they might have in their research.

A condensed and scannable fan chart view allowed users to easily spot any missing pieces within their past.

Reception was great and engagement sky rocketed as all tree views were exposed to all users. The positive buzz these releases were creating in the geneaology community showed a great increase in new users subscribing and importing their family trees from competitor tools.
We looked back at our personas’ usage metrics...
The Novice
First few sessions in the new editor showed a great increase in tree sizes. Users were adding more relatives and more information, and as such, they were getting more automatic matches. Their trees were growing quickly, and so was their engagement as more and more of these users were becoming ‘Family Explorers’.
The Family Explorer
Medium sized tree owners were breaking their research wall, as number of weekly visits increased dramatically. They were using the historical records search engine 40% more, and purchasing 120% more DNA kits to dive deeper into their research. This group showed the largest increase in users becoming paying members.
The Genealogist
Engagement increased substantially, as a lot of new information was being added by tree owners. They were perfecting the information they already had by easily finding gaps and errors using the Consistency Checker, contacing other related tree owners and researching specific groups within their tree.
And most importantly, along this process, people’s lives were changed.
People were finding lost relatives, families were being reunited, exciting facts and documents were being discovered...
Our mission, for now, had come to an end.
